Guide to Writing Course Descriptions

Each academic program should craft brief descriptions within the framework of the following requirements and editorial standards.

Requirements
  1. Make no reference to quantifiable attributes noted in separate Banner fields including title, credits, prerequisites, corequisites, enrollment restrictions, and ULR and MLO satisfaction.
  2. Limit each description to no more than 500 characters including spaces (about 75 words). To prevent truncation when pasted to Banner, Marketing & Publications will shorten descriptions that exceed the character limit. Though every effort will be made to return to the academic program for review, timelines relative to student planning and registration may dictate otherwise.
  3. Include a parenthetical indication of how often the course is offered at the end of the description, e.g. (Offered every semester.); (Offered fall semester.); (Offered spring semester.); (Offered fall semester of even years.); (Offered fall semester of odd years.); (Offered spring semester of even years.); (Offered spring semester of odd years.); (Offered only as interest warrants.). This frequency indicator does not count against the 500-character limit for the description itself.
Editorial Standards
  1. Write in present tense. (e.g. "Students complete…" rather than "Students will complete…")
  2. Write in active voice with the course as the understood (i.e. unstated) subject. (e.g. "Covers…" rather than "…are covered in this course.")
    ~ Use active verbs such as includes, discusses, provides, requires, and focuses.
    ~ Fragments caused by leaving out the understood subject are okay.
  3. Write terse sentences where every word delivers meaning.
    ~ Deliver what students want to know before enrolling in a course.
    ~ Briefly cover specifics of content, pedagogy, and outcomes.
    ~ Avoid compound sentences.
    ~ Avoid use of obvious words (e.g. students, professor , this class)
Example

Before Applying Editorial Standards

HSP 651

This course explores the complexity of health policy analysis and advocacy. Major health policy issues will be discussed including 1) rapidly changing roles of and expectations for institutions and health care providers; 2) interface between the public health and private medical sectors, and 3) a comparative analysis of the publicly and privately financed healthcare systems. A critical and historical analysis of US health policy development and a comparative analysis of non-US health systems and policies will inform the development of a health policy analysis assignment.

After Applying Editorial Standards

HSP 651

Explores the complexity of health policy analysis and advocacy in the context of 1) rapidly changing roles and expectations for healthcare institutions and providers; 2) the interface between public health and the private medical sector; and 3) a comparative analysis of publicly and privately financed U.S. and non-U.S. healthcare systems. Critical and historical analyses of U.S. and non-U.S. health policy form the basis of a health policy analysis assignment. (Offered spring semester.)
